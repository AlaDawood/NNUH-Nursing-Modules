The framework of bones and cartilage that protects us, and allows us to move comprises the skeletal system.  The tissues associated with the skeletal system include the following:  bone, cartilage, dense connective, epithelium, blood, adipose, and nervous tissue.

 

Functions of the skeletal system:

Did you know that the average skeleton weighs
22-25 pounds?

  1. Support: helps keep the body off the ground

 

  1. Protection: of vital organs and structures

 

  1. Movement: muscles need to attach to something sturdy to bring about quick and powerful movement

 

  1. Mineral storage:  Such as calcium and phosphorus, which can be distributed to parts of the body on demand.

 

  1. Blood cell production:  Red bone marrow produces blood cells by the process of “hemopoiesis.”

 

  1. Energy storage:  Lipids in the adipose tissue of yellow bone marrow is an important source of chemical energy.

 

Types of bones:

 

The bones of the body can be classified into four principal shapes:

 

  1. Long bones:  Made primarily of compact bone with spongy bone toward the ends, includes the bones of the thighs, legs, toes, arms, forearms and fingers.

 

  1. Short bones:  Somewhat cube-shaped, nearly equal in length and width, made of spongy bone except thin surface layer of compact bone; includes the wrist and ankle bones.

 

  1. Flat bones:  Generally two thin parallel plates of compact bone enclosing a layer of spongy bone, provide protection and large surface area for muscle attachment; includes the bones of the cranium, sternum, ribs and scapulas.

 

  1. Irregular bones:  Complex shapes, cannot be grouped in the other three categories; includes the facial bones and vertebrae.  

 

*Two additional types of bones are classified by location:

“Sutural bones” (AKA wormian bones) are small bones in the cranium between the joints (sutures).

 

“Sesamoid bones” are located in tendons such as the wrist.  Number of bones varies greatly.  The patella (kneecaps) are sesmoid bones everyone has.

A few highlights from “bone formation”

and bone “growth and maintenance”

 

“Ossification”…The process by which bone forms.  The human embryo skeleton is composed of connective tissue and hyaline cartilage.  Ossification begins about week six and continues through adulthood.

 

Even in an adult, old bone tissue is replaced by new tissue by the process called  “remodeling.”  Bone is never metabolically at rest, it constantly remodels.

 

Proper bone growth is dependent upon certain factors:

 

Diet: Sufficient quantities of calcium and phosphorus are needed.  So are vitamins D (for calcium absorption), and vitamin C for healthy bone matrix.

 

Hormones:  Human growth hormone influences bone growth; the sex hormones testosterone and estrogens bring about growth during puberty.

 

Exercise:  Mechanical stress can alter bone size and strength (within limits).

 

Aging:  With aging comes a loss of calcium from bones.  In females, it begins at thirty, accelerates greatly at age 40-45 as estrogen decreases.  (Thirty percent of bone calcium is lost by age 70)  In males, calcium loss begins around age 60.

    1. Meatus:  A tube like passageway running within a bone

 

    1. Paranasal Sinus:  An air-filled cavity within a bone connected to the nasal cavity

 

    1. Fossa:  A depression in or on a bone

 

  1. Processes that form joints:

 

    1. Tuberosity:  A large, rounded, usually roughened process

 

    1. Spinous process:  A sharp, slender process of a vertebra

 

    1. Trochanter:  A large, blunt process found only on the femur

 

    1. Crest: A prominent border or ridge

The vertebral column:

 

The vertebral column supports the head, protects the spinal cord and allows for ribs and back muscles to attach.

 

 

The adult vertebral column contains 26 vertebrae.  (fig. 6.13)

 

7 Cervical (in the neck)

12 Thoracic (in the chest region)

5 Lumbar  (in the low back)

5 Sacral  (Fused into one bone called the sacrum)

4 Coccygeal  (fused into the coccyx)

 

*Prior to the fusion of the sacrum and the coccyx, there are 33 vertebrae.

 

Between vertebrae, from the first vertebrae (the atlas) to the sacrum are “intervertebral discs.”

The Thorax:

 

Anatomically, the thorax refers to the entire chest.  The skeletal portion of the thorax is formed by the sternum, costal cartilage, ribs and the bodies of the thoracic vertebrae.  (Fig. 6.18)

 

The sternum is divided into three parts:

 

Upper part:   The manubrium

Middle part:   The body

Lower part: the xiphoid process (a piece of cartilage provides attachment for some abdominal muscles)

 

The sternum houses red bone marrow throughout life.  It is a common site for withdrawal of marrow for biopsy.  This procedure is called “sternal puncture.”

There are 12 pairs of ribs. The first 7 increase in length and are called “true ribs” because they attach directly to the sternum by way of costal cartilage.

 

The 5 lower remaining ribs are called “false ribs.”  Their costal cartilages do not attach directly to the sternum.

 

The lowest 2 pairs of false ribs are also called floating ribs because they do not attach to the sternum at all.

 

Each of the 12 ribs articulates with the corresponding 12 thoracic vertebrae.

 

Pectoral (shoulder) Girdle:

 

The shoulder girdle attaches the arms to the axial skeleton.  Each shoulder girdle has two bones:  (Fig. 6.21-23)

 

The upper arm has one bone:  The humerus

The lower arm has two bones:  The radius and ulna

The wrist has eight bones:  The carpals arranged in two rows

The palm has five bones:  The metacarpals

The fingers have 14 bones:  The phalanges, three in each finger except the thumb has two.

 

The Pelvic (hip) Girdle:

 

The hip girdle consists of two hipbones (AKA coxal bones).  The two hipbones articulate in the front at a fibrocartilaginous joint called the “pubic symphysis.”  They articulate at the back with the sacrum.
 
The two hipbones, the sacrum and coccyx form a basin-like structure called the “pelvis.”

 

Each hipbone has three regions:  The ilium, the ishium and the pubis (fig. 6.25)

 

 

 

 

The lower extremity  (leg): (fig. 6.26)

 

Each leg (with foot) has 30 bones.  

The upper leg has one bone:  The femur

The knee cap has one bone:  The patella

The lower leg has two bones:  The tibia and fibula

The ankle has seven bones:  The tarsals (The sole has 5 metatarsals)

The toes have 14 bones:  The phalanges (arranged, and numbered just like the bones of the fingers)

Comparison of the male and female skeleton:

 

The bones of the male are generally larger and heavier than the female.

 

The points of attachment for larger muscles show more prominence in males.  Ex. Tuberosities, lines, ridges
